Class Structure

Each class will have three parts:

1. Lecture

Each class will begin with a brief (10-30 minute) presentation that motivates the material to be covered in class each day. See placeholder for slides.

2. Discussion

After the lecture, we will discuss the assigned readings for that week. 3 students will serve as "discussion leaders" (see Grading and Assignments for more details on what being a discussion leader entails) for that week's readings. The discussion will last from 20 minutes up to an hour depending on the readings.

It is possible to choose the week that you will lead the discussion at the following link: Discussion Leader Sign Up.

3. Workshops!

The last hour to 90 minutes of each class will be devoted to a workshop. In the workshop students will work in groups to apply data analysis, visualization, and storytelling skills to the topic of the week. See workshops for a list of workshops.

Requirements

This course has absolutely 0 pre-requisites, and only one requirement -- that students have a laptop that they can bring to class each week. We will be using these laptops to binge watch Game of Thrones. Just kidding.

We'll be using the laptops to complete the workshops.

Because students in this course have a wide range of experience, workshops are only jumping off points for your personal investigations. I will teach the necessary skills to complete each workshop, but if you already are familiar with the techniques to be covered, feel free to begin without me.

For this course we will be using the R Project for Statistical Computing for our workshops. No programming experience is necessary, and a gentle introduction to R will be covered in the course see R Resources for more info on getting R set up on your laptop and learning the basics.

Grading and Assignments

The course grade components are :

  • 10% Infographic

  • 20% Journalism Piece

  • 40% Final Project

  • 15% Discussion Leadership & Participation

  • 15% Workshop Participation

Grading Details

1. Infographic

The infographic is a basic visual representation of an interesting relationship found in health data. It will be assigned at the end of the second lecture 9/15/14 and is due two weeks later 9/29/14. The infographic will be a simple one page document highlighting health disparities in the United States. It will serve as an introduction to both analysis and data visualization. You will use data from the Dartmouth Health Atlas (or a similar source, such as County Health Rankings) to construct their infographic.

2. Data driven journalism piece.

The journalism piece will use public access Medicare cost data and will consist of you guys writing an article similar in style to Elizabeth Rosenthalâ€™s great series of New York Times articles a good example and all of her work. It is to be no more than 2 pages in length, and feature no more than two 2 visualizations. This will be assigned in the fifth week of the course 10/6/14 and is due two weeks later 10/20/14

3. Final Project

The final project will consist of a policy analysis or data driven journalism piece. It will be at least 8 pages in length and include at least 3 visualizations. You must provide a rough draft a week before their presentation and give a 20-30 minute presentation of their work. The final projects will be assigned in the 8th week of the course 10/27/14 and be due whenever the final exam date for the course is. The last two classes will consist of students presenting their projects.

4. Discussion Leadership and Participation

You are required to sign up to lead at least 3 discussions each. Leading a discussion entails reading and motivating the discussion for that weeks assigned papers. Discussion Leader Sign Up.

5. Workshop Participation

Basically, you're required to participate in the workshops by completing the basic exercises assigned for each week.
